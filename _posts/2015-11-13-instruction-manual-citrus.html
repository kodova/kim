Although citrus can be found year round, from November through January is when it is at its peak here in Arizona.  Because many of us have citrus trees (or our friends or family do) I thought an Instruction Manual on citrus would be helpful!  If you are outside of the few states that grow citrus, it is still important to know how to buy, store, and prepare your lemons, limes, oranges, grapefruit, etc.

If you currently use bottled lemon or lime juice, taste it and notice the strange flavor it has.  If it doesn't taste strange to you, taste it next to fresh citrus juice.  When lemon juice is bottled it is watered down and sugar and preservatives are added.  It may stay for a long time in your fridge, but consider how long those toxins might stay in your body!  Using fresh citrus will also immediately improve the flavors in the dishes you cook.

Buying or Picking Citrus</h2>

  • Avoid blemishes or bruises or ones that are soft and spongy - the flesh may be damaged.</li>
  • Look for ones that are heavy for their size (pick two same size and feel for differences, repeat until you understand what is heavy and light - this will eventually become second nature).</li>
  • Larger citrus are likely to have more juice than smaller ones - the rind on small lemons tend to be thicker than larger ones leaving you with less flesh.</li>
  • Organic or Conventional? If you do not plan to eat the peel then it is ok to buy conventional as the peel protects the inner flesh.  Buy organic if you plan to zest or use the peel for other uses. If organic is not available or in your budget then use a produce wash</a> to clean them.</li>
    </ul>

    Storing Citrus</h2>

    • You can keep citrus on your countertop for about a week, however it will start to loose moisture and start to shrivel.</li>
    • For longer storage, place in a zip top bag in your fridge for a month (do not use the produce bags from the grocery store as they are too thin and do not provide as good of a seal).</li>
    • Zest citrus and store in a zip top bag in your freezer for about 6 months.</li>
    • Juice your citrus, place into ice cube trays, freeze, and transfer to a zip top bag.</li>
      </ul>

      Tools for Citrus</h2>

      • You can simply use tools you already have - the side of a box grater or a vegetable peeler can be used to remove the zest of the citrus, a fork can be inserted into the flesh and twisted to juice.  You can also use your hands or place the citrus half in the middle of tongs to squeeze out juice.</li>
      • There are plenty of options for citrus juicers</a> - I typically use a wood reamer like this one</a>.</li>
      • There are also plenty of options for zesting</a>, find one that is most appealing to you as they all have positive uses.</li>
        </ul>

        Tips</h2>

        • When zesting citrus, avoid the white pith as it is bitter.</li>
        • Be sure to remove the seeds by straining through your fingers or a strainer - seeds also add bitterness.</li>
        • Always zest before juicing! It is easier to remove the zest while the fruit is still fully in tact.</li>
        • Before juicing, roll the citrus between the palm of your hand and the counter to help loosen the juice.</li>
        • Grill either slices to halves for a unique, smokey, and sweet flavor.</li>
        • Zest can add a large punch of flavor without the acid.</li>
        • Use as an acid in a salad dressing (see my Salad Dressing Instruction Manual </a>for ideas).</li>
        • Mandarines and Tangerines are great snacks for kids</a> or lunches.</li>
        • Navel Oranges are good for eating, Valencia are great for juicing (but have a stringy unpleasant texture).</li>
        • Pink or Ruby Red Grapefruit are best for eating straight.</li>
          </ul>
