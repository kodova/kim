Pancakes are something that everyone should know how to make. You can make a simple pancake topped with butter and maple syrup or complicate it with fancy toppings.

To make pancakes you simply mix a bunch of dry ingredients in one bowl and then a bunch of wet ingredients in another, then gently mix them together.  But there are things that can go wrong.

To get started you need to know that pancakes have a leavening agent so they rise, giving them a light fluffy texture.  Dense pancakes are a sign that something went wrong with your baking soda.  Over time your baking soda can loose its effectiveness.  If you aren't sure how long your baking soda had been in your pantry then do this quick test:  Take a small amount (about 1/4 teaspoon or so) of baking soda in a small bowl and add a couple drops of vinegar to it.  You should see it foam vigorously.

If your baking soda is good and you still have dense pancakes then look over your other ingredients to see if you have an acid.  Buttermilk is a common acid used in pancakes, so if you thought you could do a straight substitute of regular milk in your recipe think again! But there are some simple ways to add acid to your pancakes.  You can add a little bit of lemon juice to your milk and let it sit on the counter for a few minutes, you can add some yogurt to your milk, or whisk cultured buttermilk in with your dry ingredients.

Many people suggest making pancake batter ahead of time that way you can make pancakes throughout the week.  This works to some extent, however as soon as the baking soda is activated, it starts to loose its power.  So for best results, use the batter immediately.

If you want to have an easier way of making pancakes then look at the recipe for an instant pancake mix.  It will yield 3 batches of pancakes (or if you like to make small batches of pancakes it can easily be halved).  All you need to do is scoop out two cups of the mix, whisk your buttermilk, eggs, and butter/oil and fold the two together.

UPDATE:</span> I have changed this recipe to use Whole Wheat Flour.  Using whole wheat flour is not just about health in this recipe.  Whole wheat flour actually makes fluffier pancakes and it isn't as important to not over mix your ingredients.  It is a little, just a little, earthier...but I personally love that and think it pairs deliciously with the syrup! And having a more fool proof and healthier recipe is so worth it!

KIM Tips for perfect pancakes</h2>

  • Mix your dry and wet ingredients separately.  Whisk your dry ingredients so the baking soda and powder are evenly distributed.  Mix the wet ingredients in a separate bowl so you don't over mix your batter.</li>
  • Let me repeat that again: Don't over mix your batter</strong>, lumps of flour are more than acceptable.  Mix just enough to moisten your dry ingredients.</li>
  • Make sure your baking soda is active or you will get flat pancakes (see instructions in the article above).</li>
  • Use a griddle or a pan with a heavy bottom to prevent burning your pancakes.</li>
  • Wait until bubbles in the batter form AND pop, leaving holes that stay open (if they fill back up then your batter hasn't been cooked enough).</li>
  • Flip your pancakes once!  If you aren't sure that they are the golden color you desire, then quickly peak underneath and flip only when they are ready.</li>
  • Don't press down on your pancakes after you have flipped them.</li>
