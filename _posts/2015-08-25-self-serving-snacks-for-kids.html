Children depend on their parents for food, but eventually that dependency needs to be broken.  You may be busy working or tending to another child  and can't prepare food every time it is demanded (breastfeeding moms know that is hard to feed two kids at once!).  You could value teaching your kids independence from a young age (Montessori educational theory) and food is one of the most basic needs that kids need to learn how to fulfill.  Or you could just be sick of your child asking for food every waking hour!

Many parents lean on prepared snacks like string cheese, packs of goldfish, fruit snacks, and the like to get their kids fed quickly.  These are also common school lunch snack, and this is the time of year that kids are starting back at school.  You may be energized enough to prepare great lunches at the start of the school year, but as the weeks go on it will get more difficult.  These snacks can easily be prepared earlier in the week and even your kids can put them in their lunch box!

Lunch box? How about lunch sack for work?  These are not just kid friendly snacks.  They are also snacks that as an adult could take with you to work as a part of a healthy work lunch!

Lets break the bad snack habit.  Lets start to teach our kids to cook</a> or simply serve themselves food.

KIM Snack Tips</h3>
First you need to clean out a spot in your fridge and pantry to keep the snacks.  For kids, this spot should be low and easy to access.  If you have a fridge with a bottom freezer, use a low shelf that they can reach (I use the lowest tray on the fridge door).  In the pantry, keep a basket (a simple one from the dollar store will do) on a low shelf or use a pull out shelf.

Next, either choose to use plastic zip top bags or easy to open containers.  If your child is young they may not be able to open a zip top bag.  In addition, consider small containers to hold hummus, peanut butter, or other dips.  I like the size of the OXO Tot Blocks</a> but you could even use empty baby food jars.  For work, choose ones that are easily stackable so they fit nicely in a lunch sack, your work fridge, or on your desk.

Set aside 15 minutes one or two days a week to prepare the snack (best time is after your shopping trips or during a dinner that has some down time).  Have your kids help by counting out pieces, using a scoop, or simply putting the lid on containers.

Some weeks your kids will eat more than others.  To prevent spoilage choose a balance of non-perishable items like nuts and dried fruit to fresh fruit, vegetables, and dairy.
