I love the idea of root to stalk and nose to tail cooking.  Using every part of a plant or animal is a great way to be mindful of the earth and also to save money.  Usually my corn husks and cobs go straight into my compost.... Not anymore!  Now they go into a pot of water to make a corn stock first.

To get the most corn flavor, first "toast" the husks.  This can be done a couple of ways.  If I am hanging outside while I have some cobs on the grill, I will place a few at a time on the upper rack of my gas grill.  You could put them directly on the grill, but make sure your heat is low or they will burn.  The grill toasts them quick, but requires you to keep a close eye on them. If you don't want to pay close attention to them, then you can lay them on a sheet pan weighted down with a cooling rack.  Bake at 350 for about 15 minutes, tossing halfway through to allow for even cooking.

Just like any other stock, vegetable, chicken</a> or beef, you can either simmer in a large stock pot for a long time or you can use a pressure cooker.  I have directions below for a standard pot since a pressure cooker is not available to everyone.  For a pressure cooker - place all ingredients in the pot, bring to pressure, let cook for 45 minutes then depressurize naturally or using the cold water method.

This stock can be made with however many corn husks and cobs you have available.  Use about one cup of water per ears of corn.  I haven't tried it, but if you only have a couple you could freeze the cobs (and possibly the husks too) and use them when you have more corn or additional vegetables for a more traditional vegetable stock.

You can grill or cook the corn before using the cob for stock.  Or you can cut the kernels off raw.

Use the stock as you would any vegetable stock.  I used it in a Corn and Chorizo soup (check back soon for the recipe!).
