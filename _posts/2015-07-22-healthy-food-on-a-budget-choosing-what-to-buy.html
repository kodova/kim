As much as I like to cook and experiment in the kitchen, I have been forced to keep a food budget or the money I spend would be out of control!  I previously</a> discussed the problem with food waste</a>.  Today I am going to help you choose what foods you should buy.

Start out by keeping a few weeks or a month of grocery receipts and see what your total spent is so you know how much you currently are spending. Then look at the top items on each receipt.  What are you spending the most money on?</em>  Is it expensive meat? Frozen dinners?  Snacks?  Once you identify those, consider how you can reduce those items.

Do you snack when you are stressed or bored?</em>  Try chewing gum, drinking a large glass of water, or brushing your teeth instead of snacking.

Consider the junk food you buy as wasted money… these foods are nutritionally void, it is just to fill your stomach.  Instead use that money to buy foods that will provide you with the nutrition your body needs (fruits, vegetables, dairy if acceptable).

Do you eat frozen dinners because you didn’t have food to cook or more than a few minutes to spend in the kitchen?</em>  Then plan over the weekend and cook in advance - I have a great Meal Planner</a> to help with that.  Find meals that you can put together quickly from your pantry.  Try freezer cooking one day a month.  There are a lot of awesome recipes that you put in a ziptop bag, freeze, then put it directly into a slow cooker - Talk about easy!

If you don’t want to plan your own weekly meals look at some magazines like Cooking Light and Clean Eating - they usually have two weeks of a meal plan in each issue.  There are also online services that do it for you like Gathered Table and emeals.

Do you spend a lot on meat?</em>  Check your portion sizes - they should be four ounces.  So for a family of 4 you would need just one pound for dinner.  Chicken breasts are a lot more than 4 ounces!

Have a goal of $ per pound - for me that is about $3.  If you have a priority of eating organic, free range, grass fed then that dollar amount will need to be more.  So if I want a NY strip steak, I need to counteract that expense with cheap chicken thighs/breasts or pork shoulder.  I also started to have meatless meals - using tofu or beans as our main protein source which are significantly cheaper.  Tofu is about 1.50/lb and a can of bean is often under $1.

I have also increased the amount of vegetables and grains that I serve with every meal - breakfast and lunch included.

You should take this same approach with vegetables as you do with meat - have a goal per pound… $1 for conventional or $2 for organic.  Anything above that should be treated as a treat if you are on a strict budget, or counteracted with more items that are cheaper.

So how do you keep within these goals?</em>

  • Buy in season or with the sales (check the fronts and backs of grocery flyers for the best deals)</li>
  • Meal plan around those sales</li>
  • Be flexible with your recipes/grocery list: Buy what is on sale - use different types of tomatoes, use different colored bell peppers, different types of greens, different fish, different pieces of chicken, cauliflower instead of broccoli.</li>
  • Do not buy more than you plan to eat this week - unless you know how to store it for the long term</li>
  • Make your grocery list and stick to it!</li>

    Dirty Dozen and Clean 15</b>

    If you don’t buy organic figure out how important buying organic is to you.  It is a subject that is still highly debated on whether or not organic produce has health benefits.  Check out the dirty dozen list</a>, pick a few of your favorite foods and start buying those organic.  Ease into it.

    We have all probably heard about these lists, but can you tell me what foods are on each of them right now?  Either when you are making your grocery list, check what items are on these lists and buy accordingly.  If you don’t make grocery lists, then print out the Dirty Dozen</a> and Clean 15</a> lists and keep them with you (they also have an app</a>!).  Don’t spend extra money on sweet potatoes when they are not on the dirty list… On the other hand, regular potatoes usually are - so know that you may want to buy those organic.

    Do your best to try to stick to dirty dozen list.  Organic items go on sale too! So if an organic item on the dirty dozen list goes on sale, take advantage of that.  Otherwise, fill your shopping cart with items that are on the clean 15 list.  There is also a rating system</a> for all foods by the EWG, including packaged foods.  It is generally safe to buy non-organic produce if it is not on the dirty dozen list.  You can also use a homemade wash to significantly reduce the amount of pesticides on your food.

    Also, know what items are not significantly more money to purchase organic.  Spinach is one of the cheapest organic foods.  You can buy a large container or bag from Costco for just a couple dollars.

     

    Fresh vs Frozen</b>

    Many items are cheaper if you buy them frozen especially if they are out of season (there are organic frozen options, too).  Berries, spinach, artichokes, brussel sprouts, etc.  You can also save a lot of money if you freeze your own during the seasonal peaks.  Right now berries and stone fruit are cheap. Blueberries, raspberries, and blackberries can be left whole.  Stem and half strawberries (or quarter larger ones), and slice stone fruit.  Place on a baking sheet, freeze for a few hours, then transfer to a zip top bag.  This is great for smoothies or muffins.

     </strong></strong>

    Canned veggies</b>

    Most Canned vegetables nutritionally are the same or similar to fresh produce.  But they add the benefit of being cheap and available in your pantry whenever.  You may not have a meal planned, but you could thaw a chicken breast and open a can of vegetables.  Need a quick snack, try some canned pears or peaches.  Tomatoes are actually one vegetable that is great canned even by professional chefs.  I make my own marinara and pizza sauce from canned tomatoes.  The large #10 cans you can find at Costco are more than half the price than if you bought the same amount of 28oz cans from a regular store.


    </strong></strong>Consider canning yourself.  Find smaller batch recipes for using the produce that is in season.  I have a personal goal of     preserving something</a> once every other month (sometimes I do a lot at once).  Which really is not a big task!  I have started making my own pickled jalapeños</a>, salsa, blueberry syrup (my son’s favorite), pepper jelly, sweet & sour pickles, tomato jam, preserved lemons.  This helps preserve vegetables when they are cheap, but it also prevents you from buying more expensive specialty items.

    Be sure to check for added sugar, salt, or preservatives when you are buying canned items.</strong>
